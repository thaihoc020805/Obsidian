Let us look at the very basics of <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">networking in Kubernetes</mark>. We will start with a single node kubernetes cluster. The <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">node </mark>has an IP address, say it is <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">192.168.1.2</mark> in this case. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">This is the IP address we use to access the kubernetes node, SSH into it etc</mark>. So on the single node kubernetes cluster we have created a Single POD. As you know a POD hosts a container. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Unlike in the docker world were an IP address is always assigned to a Docker CONTAINER</mark>, in <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Kubernetes</mark> the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">IP address is assigned to a POD</mark>. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">Each POD in kubernetes gets its own internal IP Address</mark>. In this case its in the range 10.244 series and the IP assigned to the POD is 10.244.0.2. So how is it getting this IP address?<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> When Kubernetes is initially configured it creates an internal private network with the address 10.244.0.0 and all PODs are attached to it</mark>. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">When you deploy multiple PODs, they all get a separate IP assigned</mark>. The<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> PODs can communicate to each other through this IP</mark>. But <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">accessing other PODs using this internal IP address MAY not be a good idea as its subject to change when PODs are recreated</mark>. We will see BETTER ways to establish communication between PODs in a while. For now its important to understand how the internal networking works in kubernetes.

![[Pasted image 20250802224511.png]]

So it’s all easy and simple to understand when it comes to networking on a single node. But how does it work when you have <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">multiple nodes in a cluster</mark>? In this case we have two nodes running kubernetes and they have IP addresses 192.168.1.2 and 192.168.1.3 assigned to them. Note that they are<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> not part of the same cluster yet.</mark> Each of them has a single POD deployed. As discussed in the previous slide these pods are attached to an internal network and they have their own IP addresses assigned. HOWEVER, if you look at the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">network addresses</mark>, you can see that they<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> are the same</mark>. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">The two networks have an address 10.244.0.0 and the PODs deployed have the same address too</mark>. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">This is NOT going to work well when the nodes are part of the same cluster</mark>. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">The PODs have the same IP addresses assigned to them and that will lead to IP conflicts in the network</mark>. Now that’s ONE problem. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">When a kubernetes cluster is SETUP, kubernetes does NOT automatically setup any kind of networking to handle these issues</mark>. As a matter of fact, <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">kubernetes expects US to setup networking to meet certain fundamental requirement</mark>s. Some of these are that<mark style="background: #FF5582A6;"> all the containers or PODs in a kubernetes cluster MUST be able to communicate with one another without having to configure NAT</mark>. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">All nodes must be able to communicate with containers and all containers must be able to communicate with the nodes in the cluster</mark>. Kubernetes expects US to setup a networking solution that meets these criteria.

![[Pasted image 20250802225419.png]]

Fortunately, we don’t have to set it up ALL on our own as there are multiple pre-built solutions available. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Some of them are the cisco ACI networks, Cilium, Big Cloud Fabric, Flannel, Vmware NSX-t and Calico</mark>. Depending on the platform you are deploying your Kubernetes cluster on you may use any of these solutions. For example, i<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">f you were setting up a kubernetes cluster from scratch on your own systems, you may use any of these solutions like Calico, Flannel etc</mark>. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">If you were deploying on a Vmware environment NSX-T may be a good option</mark>. I<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">f you look at the play-with-k8s labs they use WeaveNet</mark>. In our demos in the course we used Calico. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Depending on your environment and after evaluating the Pros and Cons of each of these, you may chose the right networking solution.</mark>

routing
- Khi Pod `10.244.0.4` (trên Node1) muốn nói chuyện với Pod `10.244.2.2` (trên Node2).
    
- Node1 nhìn vào "bản đồ" do Calico vẽ và biết rằng: "À, địa chỉ `10.244.2.2` nằm trong dải `10.244.2.0`, muốn đến đó thì phải gửi gói tin qua Node2 có địa chỉ IP vật lý là `192.168.1.3`".
    
- Node1 sẽ đóng gói gói tin và gửi nó qua mạng vật lý đến Node2. Node2 nhận được và chuyển tiếp đến đúng Pod `10.244.2.2`.
